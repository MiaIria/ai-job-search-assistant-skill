# Workflow Methodology

## Problem

Before applying for jobs, candidates often spend most of their time on scattered
research:

- searching listings across platforms,
- deduplicating company names,
- checking whether a role actually fits,
- researching culture and compensation,
- adapting resume wording,
- preparing company-specific interview answers.

The problem is not only information volume. The bigger problem is that every
step is usually done with a different mental model.

## Design Choice

The skill uses a five-step funnel:

```text
50 companies found
-> 20-25 priority companies
-> 15 deep research targets
-> 8 strongest application targets
-> final report and action plan
```

This keeps the user in control. Each stage has an inspectable output before the
next stage begins.

## Step Rationale

### Step 1: Search And Screen

Goal: build a broad but relevant market map.

The output is intentionally wide, because early filtering can miss good
opportunities.

### Step 2: Resume Parsing And Fit Scoring

Goal: turn the candidate's real background into a structured profile and score
the initial company list.

This prevents recommendations from being based only on company attractiveness.

### Step 3: Company Deep Research

Goal: understand whether the company is worth the candidate's time.

Research includes business, culture, working model, compensation, and AI team
context.

### Step 4: Deep Fit And Application Strategy

Goal: turn research into company-specific application moves.

This is where resume focus, STAR stories, interview questions, and reverse
questions become personalized.

### Step 5: Final Report

Goal: consolidate fragmented research into an action-oriented document that can
be reused.

The appendix includes researched company names so future runs can exclude them.

## Product Principle

The skill does not replace the user's judgment. It compresses research time and
makes tradeoffs visible.

## Interview Talking Point

I designed this as a funnel because job search decisions should be progressively
refined. A one-shot recommendation would hide too much uncertainty.
